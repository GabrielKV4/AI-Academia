# Phase 1: Research Justification

The ADHD compliance framework is grounded in peer-reviewed research across three domains:

1. ADHD executive function research  
2. Cognitive Load Theory  
3. Evidence-based learning science  

---

## ADHD and Working Memory

Individuals with ADHD frequently exhibit impairments in working memory and executive functioning (Martinussen et al., 2005; Barkley, 2015). Working memory limitations increase susceptibility to overload when processing long or dense textual material.

To operationalize these findings, the system enforces:

- Maximum sentence length (≤ 20 words)
- Paragraph length constraints (≤ 80 words)
- Structured content segmentation

These constraints reduce working memory overload and support sustained attention.

---

## Cognitive Load Theory

Cognitive Load Theory distinguishes between intrinsic, extraneous, and germane load (Sweller, 1988; Sweller et al., 1998). Poor formatting, large text blocks, and split-attention effects increase extraneous load and impair learning.

The system addresses this by enforcing:

- Paragraph length limits
- Formula separation rules
- Mandatory bullet structuring
- Clear section segmentation

These reduce unnecessary extraneous cognitive load.

---

## Testing Effect and Active Recall

Retrieval practice significantly improves long-term retention (Roediger & Karpicke, 2006; Karpicke & Blunt, 2011). Structured recall prompts strengthen encoding and consolidation processes.

The system requires:

- At least two recall questions per output
- A dedicated recall section

This ensures inclusion of evidence-based retrieval mechanisms.

---

## Reading Accessibility

Text readability influences cognitive accessibility. Lower syntactic and lexical complexity improves comprehension and reduces cognitive strain (Crossley et al., 2008).

The system enforces a maximum Flesch-Kincaid Grade Level of 8 to align with accessibility best practices.
